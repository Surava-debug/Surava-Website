Why the Strait of Hormuz Matters More Than OPEC

Market Insights #2 · March 23, 2026 · Surava Capital

When investors think about oil prices, they think about OPEC meetings, production quotas, and Saudi Arabia's next move. That's understandable, but it misses the bigger picture.

As we covered in our last issue, the Strait of Hormuz is the world's most critical energy chokepoint. Today we want to make a bolder argument: in terms of market impact, it matters more than OPEC ever will. And right now, that argument is playing out in real time.

What is OPEC, and why does everyone watch it?

OPEC, the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries, is a cartel of 13 major oil-producing nations including Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, and Iran. Together with allied producers like Russia under the broader OPEC+ framework, they collectively control around 40% of the world's oil output. Their primary tool is simple: agree to pump more or less oil to influence the global price. When OPEC cuts production, prices tend to rise. When they open the taps, prices fall. For decades, their meetings have been must-watch events for energy traders and investors alike.

OPEC's influence operates on a slow dial. Production cuts are negotiated, telegraphed, and often only partially implemented. Markets price them in weeks before they happen.

A chokepoint disruption is the opposite. It's sudden, unpredictable, and binary. And critically, it doesn't even require conflict to cause chaos.

A century of chokepoints

The Suez Canal alone has proven this three times over. In 1956 and again in 1967, geopolitical crises forced it shut for months and years respectively, redrawing global shipping routes and demonstrating that physical geography can override any political agreement on supply. Then in 2021, a single container ship ran aground and blocked the canal for six days with no geopolitics involved whatsoever, stranding an estimated $9 billion in goods per day. The pattern is consistent across a century: when a chokepoint closes, the world economy feels it immediately.

At Hormuz, the stakes are higher. During the 1980s Tanker War, both sides of the Iran-Iraq conflict targeted oil vessels in the Persian Gulf, forcing the U.S. to deploy naval escorts just to keep oil moving. In 2019, tanker attacks sent oil prices sharply higher within hours, not because supply had been cut, but because markets priced in the risk that it could be. No OPEC meeting has ever moved markets that fast.

This is no longer hypothetical

As of the time of writing, the Strait of Hormuz is effectively blocked. Following US-Israeli military strikes on Iran in late February 2026, Iran's Revolutionary Guard declared the strait closed and began attacking vessels attempting to transit. Tanker traffic dropped by approximately 70%, with over 150 ships anchoring outside the strait to avoid the risk. Oil prices jumped nearly 10% within days, with analysts projecting further surges toward $100 per barrel if the closure is prolonged. A US Defence Intelligence Agency assessment concluded that Iran could potentially keep the passage shut for anywhere from one to six months.

In response, OPEC+ pledged modest output increases, but the scale of the disruption far exceeds anything a production adjustment can offset. This is precisely the point: when the chokepoint closes, OPEC becomes largely irrelevant.

What this means for markets

These events create both dangers and opportunities across multiple asset classes. Energy equities react immediately. Upstream producers and integrated majors typically benefit from price spikes, while refiners and airlines face sudden margin pressure.

Volatility jumps sharply. Energy-specific volatility indices tend to spike in these episodes, historically creating dislocations that active managers watch closely.

Safe-haven assets including gold and the Swiss franc see increased demand as risk-off sentiment takes hold. There is a counterforce, though: if surging oil prices drive broader inflation higher, central banks may respond with interest rate hikes or signal tighter monetary policy. Rising real interest rates increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding assets like gold, creating downward pressure that can partially offset the geopolitical safe-haven bid. This tug-of-war between fear-driven demand and rate-driven headwinds is a dynamic worth watching. Historically, precious metals and franc exposure have acted as portfolio stabilisers in environments like this, though that same tug-of-war means their behaviour is less clean-cut than in a straightforward risk-off scenario.

U.S. Treasuries present a similar dynamic. Short-duration bonds may benefit from a flight to safety, while longer-dated bonds remain sensitive to inflation expectations and rate trajectory.

Shipping and defence names also tend to draw attention during prolonged tension. Tanker operators, naval defence contractors, and energy infrastructure providers have historically outperformed meaningfully in these environments.
