The Iran Conflict and Global Oil Markets

Market Insights #1 — March 16, 2026 · Surava Capital

AIS vessel tracking near the Strait of Hormuz — red markers show tankers anchored or waiting rather than transiting the strait, clustering at the entrance instead of continuing toward global markets. Source: MarineTraffic.

On February 28, US and Israeli forces launched joint air strikes on Iran. Within 72 hours, tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — the world's most critical oil chokepoint — had effectively halted.

Brent crude, which opened the year around $63 per barrel, briefly touched $120. This is not a normal oil spike.

The Strait carries roughly 20% of global oil demand. Every day it remains closed, roughly 17 million barrels of supply fail to reach markets, while around 7 million barrels per day of Middle Eastern production has already gone offline simply because there is nowhere to send it. The oil market operates with extremely small supply buffers — remove just a few million barrels per day from the system and prices move aggressively to rebalance supply and demand.

But fundamentals alone do not explain every move.

Fundamental vs. narrative drivers

Presidential statements, press briefings, and off-the-cuff remarks are amplifying that volatility to an extraordinary degree. They are creating price swings that no traditional supply-and-demand model would ever predict. Separating signal from noise is now one of the most important analytical challenges in energy markets. Narrative is acting as an accelerant on top of a fire that is already burning.
